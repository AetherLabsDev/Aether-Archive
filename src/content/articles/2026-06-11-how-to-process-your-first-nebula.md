Capturing your first nebula is an incredible feeling. After spending hours setting up your equipment, polar aligning, focusing, and collecting data, you finally have a folder full of images. Then reality hits, the nebula is barely visible.

Don't panic.

Every stunning nebula image you've seen started out looking faint, noisy, and disappointing. Astrophotography processing is where your data comes to life.

**Step 1: Organise Your Files**

Before opening any software, sort your images into folders:

Lights (your actual nebula exposures)

Darks

Flats

Biases (if used)

Keeping everything organised will make processing far easier and help avoid mistakes later.

**Step 2: Stack Your Images**

A single exposure contains very little signal. By combining dozens or even hundreds of images, you can dramatically reduce noise and reveal faint details.

Popular stacking software includes:

DeepSkyStacker (Windows)

Siril

PixInsight

Load your calibration frames and light frames, then run the stacking process.

When finished, don't be surprised if the result still looks grey and washed out. That's normal.

**Step 3: Stretch the Data**

Your stacked image contains far more information than your monitor can display.

Stretching increases the visibility of faint nebula details hidden within the data.

Most astrophotography software includes tools such as:

Histogram Transformation

Auto Stretch

Curves

Apply small stretches gradually rather than making huge adjustments all at once.

**Step 4: Remove Gradients**

Light pollution, moonlight, and atmospheric effects often create uneven backgrounds.

Gradient removal tools can help create a smooth, natural-looking sky background.

Popular tools include:

Siril Background Extraction

GraXpert

PixInsight Dynamic Background Extraction

Removing gradients early makes later processing much easier.

**Step 5: Adjust Colour**

Nebulae often appear dull before colour calibration.

Depending on your target, you may notice:

Red hydrogen emission regions

Blue reflection nebulae

Golden star fields

Use colour calibration tools to create a balanced and natural appearance before increasing saturation.

**Step 6: Reduce Noise**

Even after stacking, some noise will remain.

Apply noise reduction carefully. Too much can remove genuine detail and create a plastic appearance.

Less is often more.

**Step 7: Enhance Detail**

Now that the image is clean and stretched, you can begin revealing structure within the nebula.

Techniques include:

Curves adjustments

Local contrast enhancement

Sharpening

Star reduction

Make small adjustments and regularly compare with the original image.

**Step 8: Final Touches**

Before exporting your image, check:

Is the background too dark?

Are stars oversaturated?

Do colours look natural?

Is there any remaining gradient?

Small tweaks at this stage can make a significant difference.

**Don't Chase Perfection**

Your first processed nebula won't look like images from NASA or experienced astrophotographers with years of practice.

That's perfectly normal.

The goal of your first processing session isn't perfection — it's learning. Every image teaches something new, and each processing attempt improves your skills.

Save your original stacked file and revisit it in a few months. You'll be amazed at how much better you become with experience.
