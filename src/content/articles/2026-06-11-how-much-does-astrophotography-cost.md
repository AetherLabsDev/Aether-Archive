One of the first questions aspiring astrophotographers ask is simple:

"How much money do I need to get started?"

The answer is both encouraging and frustrating: astrophotography can cost anywhere from £0 to well over £20,000.

The good news is that you don't need an observatory or a bank loan to capture your first image of the night sky.

Let's break down what you can expect to spend at different budget levels.

**The £0–£100 Beginner Setup**

If you already own a smartphone, you may not need to spend anything at all.

With a tripod and a smartphone camera, you can capture:

The Moon

Star trails

Constellations

Bright planets

Milky Way landscapes

At this stage, your focus should be learning the night sky, camera settings, and basic image processing.

Typical costs:

Tripod: £20–£50

Smartphone adapter: £10–£30

Total: £0–£100

The £300–£800 DSLR Setup

This is where many astrophotographers begin.

A used DSLR paired with a tripod and a simple lens can produce surprisingly impressive images.

You can photograph:

The Milky Way

Star trails

Wide-field constellations

Bright nebulae

Typical costs:

Used DSLR: £200–£500

Lens: £100–£300

Tripod: £50–£100

Total: £300–£800

**The £800–£2,000 Star Tracker Setup**

For many people, this is the sweet spot.

Adding a star tracker allows the camera to follow the motion of the sky, enabling much longer exposures.

Suddenly, deep-sky astrophotography becomes possible.

You can photograph:

Orion Nebula

Andromeda Galaxy

North America Nebula

Rosette Nebula

Typical costs:

DSLR or mirrorless camera

Star tracker

Telephoto lens

Tripod

Total: £800–£2,000

This level of equipment is capable of producing images that many people would struggle to distinguish from those captured using far more expensive setups.

**The £2,000–£5,000 Dedicated Deep-Sky Setup**

This is where astrophotography becomes a serious hobby.

Most enthusiasts eventually move toward:

Equatorial mount

Small refractor telescope

Astronomy camera

Guiding system

The improvements include:

Better tracking accuracy

Longer exposures

Improved image quality

Greater automation

Typical costs:

Mount: £800–£2,000

Telescope: £500–£1,500

Astronomy camera: £500–£1,500

Guide system: £150–£500

Total: £2,000–£5,000

**The £5,000–£20,000+ Advanced Setup**

At this level, equipment begins to resemble a small scientific observatory.

Astrophotographers may add:

Premium mounts

Larger telescopes

Mono cameras

Filter wheels

Motorised focusers

Permanent observatories

The gains become increasingly incremental, but dedicated enthusiasts often value every improvement.

Total: £5,000–£20,000+

**The Hidden Costs**

Equipment is only part of the story.

Other expenses may include:

Software

Power supplies

Dew heaters

Filters

Travel

Storage drives

Replacement cables and accessories

These costs can add up over time.

The Biggest Myth About Astrophotography

Many beginners believe they need expensive equipment before they can start.

They don't.

Knowledge and experience matter far more than gear.

A skilled astrophotographer with a modest setup will often outperform a beginner using equipment worth thousands of pounds.

Learn the basics first. Upgrade only when your current equipment genuinely limits what you want to achieve.

**So, How Much Does Astrophotography Really Cost?**

The honest answer is:

As much or as little as you want it to.

You can capture your first image of the Moon for almost nothing.

You can build a backyard observatory for the price of a family car.

Most people find themselves somewhere in the middle, gradually upgrading over time as their skills and interests grow.

The best astrophotography setup isn't the most expensive one.

It's the one that gets you outside under the stars.
