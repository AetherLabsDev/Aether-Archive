**Introduction**

**Deep sky astrophotography is the process of photographing objects beyond our solar system, including galaxies, nebulae, and star clusters. While the stunning images you see online may look difficult to achieve, getting started is more accessible than many beginners realise.**

**With a camera, a tracking mount, and some patience, it's possible to capture light that has travelled thousands or even millions of years across space before reaching your sensor.**

**In this guide, you'll learn the equipment you need, how to choose your first targets, and the basic workflow used by astrophotographers around the world.** 



**What Is Deep Sky Astrophotography?**

**Unlike landscape astrophotography, which combines the night sky with a foreground scene, deep sky astrophotography focuses entirely on distant celestial objects.**

**Common deep sky targets include:**

**• The Andromeda Galaxy (M31)**

**• The Orion Nebula (M42)**

**• The Pleiades (M45)**

**• The North America Nebula**

**• The Whirlpool Galaxy (M51)**

**These objects are often invisible to the naked eye but can be revealed through long exposures and image processing.**



**The Equipment You Need**

**One of the biggest misconceptions about astrophotography is that you need a huge telescope and thousands of pounds worth of equipment.**

**While advanced setups certainly exist, many beginners start with a simple and affordable kit.**

**Camera**

**A DSLR or mirrorless camera is an excellent place to begin.**

**Popular beginner options include:**

**• Canon 600D**

**• Canon 1100D**

**• Nikon D5300**

**• Sony A6000**

**The most important thing is having a camera capable of shooting RAW images.**

**Tracking Mount**

**A tracking mount is arguably the most important piece of astrophotography equipment.**

**As Earth rotates, stars appear to move across the sky. A tracking mount follows this movement, allowing your camera to take long exposures without producing star trails.**

**Popular beginner trackers include:**

**• Sky-Watcher Star Adventurer GTi**

**• iOptron SkyGuider Pro**

**• Move Shoot Move Nomad**

**Lens or Telescope**

**Many beginners achieve fantastic results using a camera lens before purchasing a telescope.**

**Recommended starting focal lengths include:**

**• 135mm**

**• 200mm**

**• 300mm**

**Alternatively, a small refractor telescope between 50mm and 70mm aperture provides an excellent entry point into deep sky imaging.**

**Tripod**

**A sturdy tripod provides a stable foundation for your setup. Even the best tracking mount will struggle if the tripod moves during an imaging session.**



**Choosing Your First Target**

**The easiest way to become frustrated with astrophotography is by choosing a difficult target too early.**

**Instead, focus on bright and forgiving objects.**

**Andromeda Galaxy (M31)**

**The nearest major galaxy to our own and one of the brightest deep sky targets available.**

**Orion Nebula (M42)**

**Often considered the perfect beginner target thanks to its brightness and colourful structure.**

**Pleiades (M45)**

**A beautiful open star cluster surrounded by faint blue reflection nebulae.**

**These targets are bright enough to produce satisfying results even with relatively simple equipment.**



**Understanding Polar Alignment**

**Polar alignment is the process of aligning your tracking mount with Earth's rotational axis.**

**Without accurate polar alignment, stars will drift during long exposures, resulting in blurry images and star trails.**

**Most tracking mounts provide one of the following methods:**

**• Polar scope**

**• Electronic polar alignment routine**

**• Mobile app assisted alignment**

**Learning to polar align accurately is one of the most valuable skills a beginner can develop.**



**Capturing Your First Images**

**A typical astrophotography session follows a straightforward workflow:**

**1. Set up the tripod and mount.**

**2. Perform polar alignment.**

**3. Attach and balance your camera.**

**4. Focus carefully.**

**5. Frame your target.**

**6. Capture multiple long exposures.**

**7. Take calibration frames.**

**8. Process your data.**

**Rather than taking a single image, astrophotographers capture many exposures and combine them together later.**

**This process is known as stacking.**



**What Is Stacking?**

**Stacking combines multiple exposures into a single image.**

**The more exposures you collect, the more signal you capture and the less visible noise becomes.**

**Benefits of stacking include:**

**• Reduced noise**

**• Increased detail**

**• Better colour reproduction**

**• Improved dynamic range**

**Popular stacking software includes:**

**• DeepSkyStacker**

**• Siril**

**• PixInsight**

**Even a basic setup can produce surprisingly impressive results when enough data is collected and stacked correctly.**



**Basic Image Processing**

**The image produced immediately after stacking often appears flat and disappointing.**

**This is completely normal.**

**Image processing is where hidden detail is revealed.**

**A basic processing workflow includes:**

**1. Stacking the data.**

**2. Removing gradients.**

**3. Colour calibration.**

**4. Stretching the image.**

**5. Noise reduction.**

**6. Sharpening details.**

**Processing is a skill in its own right and can dramatically improve your final images.**



**Common Beginner Mistakes**

**Buying Too Much Equipment**

**Many beginners assume better equipment automatically produces better images.**

**In reality, mastering the fundamentals often produces larger improvements than expensive upgrades.**

**Poor Polar Alignment**

**Even a small alignment error can ruin long exposures.**

**Take your time during setup.**

**Ignoring Focus**

**Precise focus is essential.**

**A Bahtinov mask can make achieving perfect focus significantly easier.**

**Choosing Difficult Targets**

**Faint galaxies and tiny nebulae can be frustrating for beginners.**

**Start with bright, well-known objects before moving on to more challenging targets.**

**Expecting Instant Results**

**Astrophotography has a learning curve.**

**Every session teaches new skills and improves future results.**



**Final Thoughts**

**Deep sky astrophotography combines astronomy, photography, and image processing into one incredibly rewarding hobby.**

**While the learning curve can seem intimidating at first, a simple camera, tracking mount, and lens are enough to begin capturing galaxies, nebulae, and star clusters from your own backyard.**

**Focus on learning the fundamentals, keep your equipment simple, and enjoy the process. The universe has plenty to offer, and every astrophotographer starts with their very first image.**
