One of the most important skills in astrophotography is polar alignment. If your star tracker isn't properly aligned with Earth's axis of rotation, stars will drift during long exposures, resulting in blurry images and ruined data.

The good news is that polar alignment is much easier than it sounds. Once you've done it a few times, the process can take less than five minutes.

In this guide, we'll walk through the complete process of polar aligning a star tracker such as the SkyGuider Pro, Star Adventurer, or similar equatorial tracking mount.

**What Is Polar Alignment?**

Earth rotates once every 24 hours. To keep stars appearing stationary in your camera frame, your star tracker must rotate at exactly the same rate as Earth.

To achieve this, the tracker must be pointed toward Earth's rotational axis.

In the Northern Hemisphere, this means aligning the tracker with a point in the sky very close to Polaris, the North Star.

This process is called polar alignment.

**Why Is Polar Alignment Important?**

Poor polar alignment is one of the most common causes of:

\- Star trailing

\- Elongated stars

\- Failed long exposures

\- Frustrating imaging sessions

The longer your focal length and exposure time, the more important accurate polar alignment becomes.

**For example:**

\- 50mm lens: fairly forgiving

\- 135mm lens: good alignment needed

\- 300mm lens: precise alignment required

\- Telescope imaging: extremely accurate alignment required

**Equipment You'll Need**

Before starting, make sure you have:

\- A star tracker

\- A sturdy tripod

\- A polar scope

\- A smartphone

\- A polar alignment app

Popular apps include:

\- Polar Scope Align

\- PS Align Pro

\- Polar Finder

These apps show the current position of Polaris relative to the true north celestial pole.

**Step 1: Set Up Your Tripod**

Place your tripod on stable ground.

Extend the legs only as much as necessary to maximize stability.

Use a spirit level if available and level the tripod as accurately as possible.

While perfect leveling isn't strictly required for polar alignment, it makes the adjustment process significantly easier.

**Step 2: Point the Tracker North**

Attach your star tracker and roughly point it toward true north.

A compass can help, but remember that magnetic north and true north are not always the same.

For now, getting within a few degrees is sufficient.

**Step 3: Set Your Latitude**

Most star trackers include a latitude adjustment scale.

Set this to your observing location.

For example:

\- London: approximately 51°

\- Manchester: approximately 53°

\- Edinburgh: approximately 56°

This places the tracker at roughly the correct angle to match Earth's rotational axis.

**Step 4: Locate Polaris**

As darkness falls, identify Polaris in the sky.

A useful trick is to locate the Big Dipper and follow the two outer stars of its bowl upward. These "pointer stars" lead directly to Polaris.

Polaris is not the brightest star in the sky, but it is easy to identify once you know where to look.

**Step 5: Open Your Polar Alignment App**

Launch your preferred polar alignment app.

The app will display where Polaris should appear inside your polar scope at that exact moment.

Many beginners make the mistake of placing Polaris directly in the center of the reticle.

This is incorrect.

Polaris sits slightly away from the true celestial pole and must be positioned according to the app's display.

**Step 6: Look Through the Polar Scope**

Turn on the polar scope illuminator if your tracker has one.

Look through the polar scope and locate Polaris.

Use the altitude and azimuth adjustment knobs to move the tracker—not the tripod—to position Polaris in the correct location shown by your app.

Take your time here.

Small adjustments make a big difference.

**Step 7: Double-Check Your Alignment**

Once Polaris is positioned correctly, recheck the app and verify nothing shifted while tightening adjustment knobs.

Many tracking problems occur because the mount moves slightly after tightening.

If necessary, make a final correction.

**Step 8: Attach Your Camera**

Now attach your camera and lens.

Balance the setup if your tracker supports balancing.

An unbalanced system places unnecessary strain on the tracker and can reduce tracking performance.

**How Accurate Does Polar Alignment Need to Be?**

The answer depends on your focal length.

For wide-field astrophotography:

\- 14mm–50mm: rough alignment often works

\- 85mm–135mm: good alignment recommended

For telescope imaging:

\- 250mm+: precise alignment becomes critical

Fortunately, most modern star trackers are surprisingly forgiving for beginners using camera lenses.

**Common Polar Alignment Mistakes**

Moving the Tripod Instead of the Mount

After locating Polaris, adjustments should be made using the tracker's altitude and azimuth controls.

Moving tripod legs often makes alignment harder.

Putting Polaris in the Center

The North Celestial Pole is not exactly where Polaris is located.

Always use a polar alignment app to determine the correct position.

Forgetting to Tighten Adjustment Knobs

Loose adjustment bolts can allow the mount to drift after alignment.

Bumping the Setup

Even a small knock can ruin an otherwise perfect alignment.

Always verify alignment after attaching heavy equipment.

**Final Thoughts**

Polar alignment can seem intimidating when you're starting out, but it quickly becomes second nature.

With a little practice, most astrophotographers can complete the process in just a few minutes.

A well-aligned star tracker allows longer exposures, sharper stars, and significantly better astrophotography results.

Master this skill early, and you'll avoid one of the most common frustrations in the hobby.
